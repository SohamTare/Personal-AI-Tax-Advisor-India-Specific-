What is TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)?

TDS, or Tax Deducted at Source, is a mechanism where a person or company paying you for a specific service or salary is required to deduct a certain amount of tax and deposit it with the government.

How does it work?

When a payment is made, such as your salary, interest, commission, or professional fees, the payer deducts a small portion of the amount as tax. This deducted amount is then deposited to the Income Tax Department on your behalf.

Who deducts TDS?

The deductor (the person making the payment) can be an individual, a company, a firm, or a co-operative society. For salaried employees, the employer is the deductor.

How does it affect me?

The tax already deducted is reflected in your Form 16 (for salaried employees) and in Form 26AS. When you file your income tax return, this TDS amount is considered a tax you have already paid. This helps in either reducing your final tax liability or, if more tax was deducted than required, claiming a refund.
